Title: How to Have a Better Mental Health?

Having a Good Mental Health

“Happiness can be found even in the darkest of times, if one only remembers to turn on the light.” —Albus Dumbledore


Here's some tips to how you can have a better mental health!

  • Having A Positive Outlook - Trying to have a positive outlook may do better for your mental health. Try to find balance between your positive and negative emotions, negative emotions can help you respond to negative emotions but you don't want these emotions to take over. Hold on to the positive emotions when you have them. And try to take a break from negative emotions, know when not to fall for rumors, get into arguments, or negatively compare you life to others.
  • Practice Gratitude - Remind yourself of things you are greatful, if possible, everyday. This can allow you to enhoy the positive emotions and experiences you had, and practicing gratitude can help you have a new outlook and life.
  • Taking Care of Your Physical Health - Maintaining you physical health can help improve your mental health because both affect your overall health. Be physically active, exercising can improve your mood and reduce feeling of stress. It's also important to getting enough sleep, because sleep affects you mood, lack of sleep can lead to being easily irritated and annoyed. Good nutrition will help you feel better not only physically but also mentally. Good nutrition can reduce stress and decrease feelings of anxiety.
  • Social Interaction - We are social creatures, it's important for us to have strong and healthy relationships with other people. Having a good support system can help against stress. Besides interacting with loved ones, like family and friends, you could find ways to get involved with your community or neighborhood.
  • Having A Sense of Life Purpose - This could be through your job, volunteering, learning new skills, or exploring your spirituality.
  • Having Healthy Coping Mechanisms - Your coping mechanisms can help you face a problems and stress.
  • Meditation - Medidation is a mind and body practice where you learn to focus your attention and awareness. Medatating have many types, including mindfulness meditation and transcendental meditation. Meditating involves a focus of attention such as a specially chosen word or set of words, an object, or your breathing.
  • Relaxation techniques are practices you do to produce your body's natural relaxation response. This slows down your breathing, lowers your blood pressure, and reduces muscle tension and stress. Relaxation techniques include progressive relaxation, guided imagery, biofeedback, where you use electronic devices to learn to control certain body functions, such as breathing, heart rate, and muscle tension. Self-hypnosis, where the goal is to get yourself into a relaxed, trance-like state when you hear a certain suggestion or see a specific cue. And deep breathing exercises, which involve focusing on taking slow, deep, even breaths.

Know when you can't handle stress and your mental health anymore, there are a lot of mental health services such as, life coaches, counciling, talk therapy, and medication (if prescribed by your doctor). There is no shame in seeking for help. Everyone has a breaking point and everyone will need some help sometime.

Title: Coping Mechanisms

Coping Mechanisms

“Honor the pain briefly, give it the deference it deserves, tell it you love it, then it will let you go.” —A.D. Aliwat


We all have ways to cope with stress in our daily lives like, exercising, meditating, yoga, sleeping, eating, and a lot more. But not all of our coping mechanisms are healthy for our physical, social, and mental health, these unhealthy coping mechanisms can provide short-term and immediate satisfaction but they can be harmful to us.

The healthy coping mechanisms are those that can calm you down without harming your health. These can include exercising, yoga, and meditating. And the unhealthy coping mechanisms can include binge-eating, excessive sleeping, and many more that can possibly harm you and your social life. Title: 7 Signs You're Dealing With Burnout

Facing Burnout

“Balance is the epiphany that you don't have to be everything to everyone, all of the time.” —Vanessa Autrey


Burnout is a state of physical, mental, and emotional exhaustion because of excessive and prolonged stress. Burnout typically occurs when we work to much, feeling overwhelmed, unable to meet demands, and being emotionally drained. When stress continues, you begin to lose interest in things you used to love.

Common signs of burnout can include constant fatigue, muscle- and headaches, detachment, and isolation. According to HelpGuide, "Stress, by and large, involves too much: too many pressures that demand too much of you physically and mentally. Burnout, on the other hand, is about not enough. Being burned out means feeling empty and mentally exhausted, devoid of motivation, and beyond caring."

Here are some tips, based on HelpGuide, to deal with burnout!

  • Turn to Trusted People — Social Interaction is a need for human beings, and a natural antidote to stress, and it's even better if you talk to someone that loves you. It's good for you to connect with your community, school, or work! It can help lessen your stress, and may even help you ease out burnout.
  • Reevaluate your Priorities — It's important to ask yourself when you're experiencing burnout, "Am I neglecting something important to me?" Take time to rediscover yourself, find something you enjoy and makes you happy! Get plenty of sleep, take a break from technology, and learn when to say "no" to your work for just a while.
  • Have a Healthy Diet — Having a healthy diet can support your mood and energy levels! Minimize your sugar and refined carbs, reduce drinking caffeine and eating unhealthy fats. Eat more Omega-3 rich foods (fatty fish like salmon) because they can give your mood a boost! Avoid nicotine and drink alcohol moderately.

Title: The Power of Gratitude

Gratitude and Joy

"Remember, being happy doesn’t mean you have it all. It simply means you’re thankful for all you have." —Unknown.


Gratitude, also called thankfulness and greatfulness, is the quality of being thankful and showing appreciation for what we have.

Expressing gratitude can heavily improve your mood and lessen rates of stress and depression. Expressing gratidude can also make you optimistic, and it can also improve your social bonds and physical health (The Mental Health Benefits of Gratitude, 2020)! Studies show that people who express gratitude can form more meaningful bonds and greater levels of happiness (Brower, 2022).

There are also studies that show having a Gratitude Journal can cultivate the attitude of gratidude and can improve mental health. People who write in gratidude journals are less likely to have toxic and negative emotions than those who dont. Gratitude can reverse our priorities and can help us be more grateful and appreciate the people and the things we do (How Gratitude Changes You and Your Brain, 2017).

Title: An Antidote to Dissatisfaction

What is Self-help?

According to the APA Dictionary of Psychology, Self-help is "a focus on self-guided, in contrast to professionally guided, efforts to cope with life problems. Self-help can involve self-reliance, in which one addresses such problems on one’s own (e.g., by reading self-help books), or it can involve joining with others to address shared concerns together, as in self-help groups."

A lot of people struggle with caring of themselves especially because of excessive work brought by the their work or school. Resources such as self-help books, magazines, and websites can help people find ways for them to care more about their mental and overall health

What are Coping Mechanisms?

Coping is defined as the thoughts and behaviors mobilized to manage internal and external stressful situations. It is a term used distinctively for conscious and voluntary mobilization of acts, different from 'defense mechanisms' that are subconscious or unconscious adaptive responses, both of which aim to reduce or tolerate stress.

When individuals are subjected to a stressor, the varying ways of dealing with it are termed 'coping styles,' which are a set of relatively stable traits that determine the individual's behavior in response to stress. These are consistent over time and across situations. Generally, coping is divided into reactive coping (a reaction following the stressor) and proactive coping (aiming to neutralize future stressors). Proactive individuals excel in stable environments because they are more routinized, rigid, and are less reactive to stressors, while reactive individuals perform better in a more variable environment.
